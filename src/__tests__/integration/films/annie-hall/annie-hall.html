 Moofish Oct 12 2025

I can't pretend like I don't love Woody Allen's self-absorbed, smarmy kvetching, and his too-proud one-liners that kill anyway, and the whole postmodern shtick: the fragmented non-linear chronology, the constant fourth wall breaks, the eye-rolling references to base philosophy and psychology and literature. It's tailor made for me, and while Woody Allen is always a convincing and relatable (and loathable) schlemihl, Diane Keaton's lively performance truly makes this film. A bit of a ditz, but one with heart, with a desire to grow, with a passion for life that Alvy Singer mostly lacks. And most importantly, she has agency and her own desires, and she realizes that Alvy, as much as she loves him, is only dragging her down. You kinda have to facepalm every time Allen speaks as he obviously puts his foot in his mouth every time... But that's kind of the point: if you spend too much time trying to be above it all, you're going to miss out on lovely, warm people--because they aren't just going to wait for you to fix yourself as you talk down to them all the while.

RIP Diane Keaton

yerblues Jun 25 2025
Vote downBookmarkVote up
Even after Woody Allen's multiple cancellations, Annie Hall remains a film that many sharp film lovers just can't quit. Allen's first foray into "serious" filmmaking finds him starring as Alvy Singer, a character not unlike himself (What are the odds?!) who is a successful comedian but relatively unlucky in love ... until he meets Annie Hall, played brilliantly by Diane Keaton. Alvy's not a man's man, and he's not classically attractive. But he uses his humor and quick wit to exude confidence. Annie is not very book-smart, but she is intuitive and spontaneous. She's Protestant and he's Jewish. She's kind of hip and he's resolutely square. And this whirlwind romance eventually fizzles out. But what makes Annie Hall such a great movie is not just the very believable romance at the heart of it. Allen creates a postmodern masterpiece by futzing about with the traditional framed or linear narratives which are the mainstay of most romantic comedies. He breaks the fourth wall constantly, talking directly to the camera. He provides commentary on his life as it is being acted out. He takes elements of slapstick comedy and slaps them atop a fairly serious and dour documentary about the rise and fall of a relatively short relationship that lacks a classic Aristotelian happy ending. Allen's postmodern wizardry, however, would mean nothing without all the funny jokes! There are some many quotable lines and memorable scenes ("I forgot my mantra!"; the subtitled sequence; the Marshall McLuhan scene; "Don't knock masturbation. It's sex with someone I love."; "Dissentary"; "Harvard makes mistakes. Kissinger taught there."; the look on Alvy's face when Annie's brother--played by Christopher Walken--is driving and talking nonchalantly about suicide) that the film transcends conventional genre categorization. Though it's not my favorite film of Allen's (it is near the top of the heap), Annie Hall sets in place his approach for a brilliant run of intellectually stimulating and often hilarious movies that lasts (arguably) until 1989's Crimes and Misdemeanors.

ocontrarian May 11 2024
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Coming back to this after a long time, and maybe right now it gets the nod over the other mid-period classic Manhattan. Watching this again, am more impressed than ever with the direction throughout. Something so relaxed and seemingly casual betrays enormous formal control. A thumbnail sketch might plausibly claim this is a humorous anatomy of a relationship but in doing that and only that (without the aching Bergo-masque of the striving and tedious Interiors) it succeeds at something more. Mid-period Allen begins with the brilliant transitional work Sleepers, with one foot planted in the puerile slapstick of the early comedies and another squarely in the Euro-auteur world of ideas Allen longed to join. And here, he finally gets the formula sorted out: throttle back the impish Mad magazine side (it comes out here primarily in Alvy Singer's curdled sexist asides) and double down on film technique cribbed from New Wave masters (Raoul Coutard should have been given a credit here). It's a winsome alchemy: the nouvelle vaguistos were equally talented at poking holes in self-involved men but Allen puts them in the shade for humor. As he will do so often in later films, Allen mocks the avatar he himself plays even as he gives him a plausible and rounded (or more rounded than in the earlier, sillier films) psychology. There's the usual kvetching and blame placed on the family unit, but the balance of the film neatly shows Alvy Singer unwittingly planting the seeds of his new relationship's destruction, through intellectual bravado masking insecurity. It's a devastating and lacerating (self?) portrait. And in Diane Keaton (whose arrival in the Allen filmic menage galvanized his turn to more serious themes and, crucially, just better movies) is the perfect foil in this folie a deux. She doesn't get nearly enough credit for being, not just a good comedic player, but a flat out great actor. And she is great here. Her stammering babble after meeting Alvy at tennis is a wonderful introduction to her character and it allows Allen to underplay the scene, working (for a change) as the straight man to Keaton's mania. And that move is important, because the balance of the film shows how the terms of the relationship recalibrate over time. The delicate rebalancing puts the initially secure Alvy on the back footand this is all handled with incredible subtlety, both on the part of the script and the leads. The creative (and different) breakings of the fourth wall work nicely throughout to comment on and deepen the main action. After the fact it all seems so obvious, but in its day, this was incredibly creative and clever. Allen is always better when he keeps his intellectual strivings and comedic excess in checkhere he seemed to really get the balance perfect.

Alvy Singer is an annoying, sardonic little fuck, but I'd be lying if I said he wasn't hilarious or that nearly every one-liner of his didn't crack me up. Annie on the other hand, is a warm and lovable person thankfully. It took me far too long to finally watch a Woody Allen film, but if the others are anything like this then I've been missing out big time. The story here is conveyed in such a fun and unique way, the script and dialogue are gold, the actors are perfect, and the film is pure charm. There's no question that this belongs among the greatest comedies ever crafted.

On it's surface this is cute and well done. It's smart and self-deprecating and actually gives Keaton the agency she deserves. It connected with me in my early 20s when I was sad and plowing through rom-coms at a rapid speed. If you sit and think about it, it's a little indulgent though. Woody writes himself as the loveable, quirky, schlubby lead that you're supposed to sympathize with. Largely it works but that doesn't mean it's not also obnoxious.

Unfortunately the main thing that stuck with me before rewatching was that that Woody Allen wrote a punchline where his friend is upset because he had to leave the bedroom where he had 16 year old twins. For those who don't know, Woody Allen married the adopted daughter of his longtime-ex. She was 35 years younger than him. He has also faced sexual abuse allegations about his own adopted daughter. Don't know what to do with this all but I'm inclined to believe he's a bad guy which really hinders a movie that is basically all about him. I'm not going to find his brand of self-conscious neuroticism charming and I didn't like this nearly as much on rewatch because of that.

My first real Woody Allen film. Midnight in Paris may be a Woody Allen film just because he directed it. But it's completely different from this one. This one was more of a Woody Allen film because it stars him and it's from his "greats" period. And I was skeptical going in. The first movie from someone is always the hardest. It's a new experience that will most likely be even better once I've seen more of his movies but, all things considered, this isn't a bad place to start.

There's not really any plot to this. It's simply Woody Allens character and Diane Keatons character intermingling. I could tell right off that I wasn't going to be a huge fan of Allens style of comedy but his character was still intriguing. I laughed but most of the greatness for me doesn't come from the humor. For me, it's the realistic depiction of a relationship. Most rom-coms feel very superficial and the problems the relationships face feel very grand and unique to a very few people. This is probably the realest depiction of a relationship I think I've ever seen in a movie. Both characters are very likable and while Allens is definitely the more hate-able of the two, his antics and his problems feel real and the arguments and quibbles they have feel real. They aren't arguing over big grand things but small, mundane things like every relationship does. It's a beautiful film and while the comedy style isn't exactly for me, I found it to be an amazing movie; real like you could pick out any couple in the world and they would compare pretty nicely and that's what made this movie amazing for me. They're not cheating or lying or trying to get back at the other, they're just themselves and they never try to push anything. Everything happens effortlessly and I think that's why this is such a great movie.

In dire need of a revisitlast I saw this was over a decade ago, when I wasnt at all sold on any of Allens purposely vexing, overly neurotic personae, least of all the ones wherein he nevertheless managed a romantic partner very clearly out of his league. (Though, yes, you might point out, Keaton and Allen did date in real life, to which I can only retort: Sometimes life is indeed stranger than fiction.) This was among the first handful of Allen films I saw, alongside the other marquee titles, before I mentally wrote him off as not for me. Years later, after somewhat masochistically deciding to rummage through the entirety of his filmography, Ive warmed up to his earlier sense of humor and self-deprecating shtick, at least as it existed and was portrayed before the millennium (worthwhile post-nineties Allen films are extremely few and far between), though I gravitated toward titles that are rarely talked about and discussed among his most revered work e.g. ZELIG; THE PURPLE ROSE OF CAIRO; INTERIORS; DECONSTRUCTING HARRY; or BROADWAY DANNY ROSE. All that is to say, I have an inkling that were I to revisit the classics that I wrote off early onmost notably MANHATTAN and ANNIE HALLmy reaction would be far more favorable, even if not quite rhapsodic. That will one day come, Im sure, but for now all I have are my dusty memories of this rom-com, a lingering aftertaste of occasionally humorous one-liners and anxious proclamations often eclipsed by a nerve-grating solipsism. (Stuff like sneezing into the box of coke or halfheartedly attempting to kill a massive spider is what splits my sides; the lobster scenes or the opening mantra - not so much.) Of course there are inspired scenes, too, like the split-screen therapy session and the truthfully subtitled balcony conversation, the likes of which were incredibly outr for the subgenre at the time. Take this rating with a grain of salt, knowing itd likely increase a bit with a more recent viewing, but would still perhaps rank well below my other Allen favorites. I prefer him when hes not attempting hopeless romanticism.
